Financial Reports — English

Welcome! This document shows a simple example of financial reports in English to help you understand the key reports that companies use.

Key Types of Reports

  • Balance Sheet
  • Income Statement
  • Cash Flow Statement
  • Statement of Changes in Equity
  • Notes to the Financial Statements (Notes)

Balance Sheet

What is this report?

The Balance Sheet shows a company's financial position at the end of a specific period. It includes:

  • Assets — Resources the company owns (e.g., Cash, Buildings, Equipment).
  • Liabilities — Debts the company owes (e.g., Bank Loans, Accounts Payable).
  • Equity — The owner's capital and retained earnings.
Formula: Assets = Liabilities + Equity

Simple Example

Assets: - Cash: 20,000 - Inventory: 30,000 - Equipment: 50,000 Liabilities: - Bank Loan: 40,000 Equity: - Owner's Capital: 60,000 (Assets = 100,000 ; Liabilities + Equity = 100,000)

Detailed Explanation

Assets are divided into:

  • Current Assets - Assets that can be converted to cash within one year (e.g., Cash, Inventory).
  • Non-Current Assets - Assets not easily converted to cash (e.g., Property, Equipment).

Liabilities are also divided into:

  • Current Liabilities - Debts to be paid within one year.
  • Long-Term Liabilities - Debts to be paid after one year.

Income Statement

What is this report?

The Income Statement shows revenue and expenses over a period (e.g., one year). The final result is a Profit or Loss.

Formula: Profit = Revenue – Expenses

Simple Example

Revenue: - Sales: 120,000 Expenses: - Cost of Goods Sold: 60,000 - Salaries: 30,000 - Rent: 10,000 Net Profit = 120,000 - (60,000+30,000+10,000) = 20,000

Detailed Explanation

The Income Statement is divided into three main parts:

  • Revenue - The total amount of money the company receives from selling goods or services.
  • Expenses - The costs the company incurs to generate revenue.
  • Net Profit - The amount remaining after deducting all expenses.

Net Profit can be distributed as:

  • Dividends - The portion of profit distributed to shareholders.
  • Retained Earnings - The profit kept in the company for reinvestment.

Cash Flow Statement

What is this report?

The Cash Flow Statement shows the net inflow and outflow of cash, categorized by three activities:

  • Operating Activities — Daily sales and payments.
  • Investing Activities — Buying or selling assets (e.g., Equipment, Property).
  • Financing Activities — Receiving or paying capital to owners or banks.

Simple Example

Operating Cash Flow: 25,000 Investing Cash Flow: -15,000 Financing Cash Flow: 5,000 Net Cash Flow = 25,000 - 15,000 + 5,000 = 15,000

Detailed Explanation

Operating Activities include:

  • Cash received from customers
  • Cash paid to suppliers and employees
  • Paying taxes and interest

Investing Activities include:

  • Buying or selling fixed assets
  • Buying or selling investments
  • Lending money or receiving loan repayments

Financing Activities include:

  • Receiving loans from banks
  • Repaying bank loans
  • Issuing stock or repurchasing shares
  • Paying dividends

Statement of Changes in Equity

What is this report?

Shows the changes in equity over a period, such as capital contributions/withdrawals, dividends, and retained earnings.

Simple Example

Beginning Equity: 40,000 + Net Profit: 20,000 - Dividends Paid: 5,000 Ending Equity = 40,000 + 20,000 - 5,000 = 55,000

Detailed Explanation

The Statement of Changes in Equity shows:

  • Beginning Equity - The amount of equity at the start of the reporting period.
  • Net Profit - The profit earned during the reporting period.
  • Dividends - The amount distributed to shareholders.
  • Other Changes - Accounting adjustments, currency translations.
  • Ending Equity - The amount of equity at the end of the reporting period.

Notes to the Financial Statements (Notes)

What are notes?

Notes provide additional details such as accounting policies, information about debt, payment schedules, and other supplementary information.

Important Note!

Notes are essential for investors and analysts to understand the calculation methods, valuations, and accounting policies used.
